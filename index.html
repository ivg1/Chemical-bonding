Covalent bonding
Covalent bonding is when two or more elements share pairs of electrons in order to achieve a full outer shell
(they will become stable and stable is good).
This type of bonding occurs only between nonmetals.

Lets see an example for 2 oxygen atoms (both nonmetal):
On the left side of the diagram, each oxygen atom has 6 electrons in its outer shell. Since an outer shell can hold a maximum of 8 electrons, this means each oxygen atom needs 2 more electrons to fill its shell.

On the other side, the 2 oxygen atoms decided to share 2 pairs of electrons. By sharing like this, they both achieve a full outer shell with 8 electrons.

Why did they share 2 pairs of electrons instead of just 1 pair?
If the oxygens only shared 1 pair of electrons (1 electron from each atom), they would each have 7 electrons in their outer shell—not enough to reach 8. So, they decided to share another pair of electrons, which allows them to achieve their goal of 8 electrons in their outer shell.

If you count up all the electrons around each oxygen atom (their own + the shared ones), you'll see that they both have 8 electrons now.
Ionic bonding
Ionic bonding is when two or more elements gain or lose electrons in order to achieve a full outer shell

This type of bonding occurs between nonmetals + metals. Since metals tend to lose electrons and nonmetals gain.

Lets see an example for sodium chloride (table salt):
On the left side of the diagram, you can see that a sodium atom has 1 electron in its outer shell. Since an outer shell needs 8 electrons to be full, sodium doesn't need this electron — it actually wants to get rid of it to have a completely full outer shell below it.

Meanwhile, the chlorine atom has 7 electrons in its outer shell and it will only nees to gain 1 electron to achieve a full outer shell.

But instead of sharing their electrons, sodium will give away its 1 useless electron to chlorine who needs it. On the right side of the diagram, they will both have full outer shells. This is called an ionic bond.
Metallic bonding
Metallic bonding is when a metal loses electrons and becomes an ion to achieve a full outer shell

This type of bonding occurs between metals + delocalised electrons. The delocalised electrons are the electrons the metal ions lost.

Lets see an example for magnesium:
On the diagram the magnesium ions are the ones labeled by "+". The delocalised electrons, also called "the sea of delocalised electrons", are the smaller circles, and they have a negative charge.

These two make a metallic bond. The magnesium has a positive charge and is attracted the delocalised electrons.

Overall, instead of exchanging electrons, the magnesium atoms lost electrons and those electrons started moving freely between the now magnesium ions.
Characteristics of these bonds
Covalent bonding
  • Low melting & boiling point
  • Dont conduct electricity
Ionic bonding
  • High melting & boiling point
  • Conduct electricity when molten/aqueous
  • Soluble in water
Metallic bonding
  • High melting & boiling point
  • Conduct electricity
  • Malleable (can be shaped)
